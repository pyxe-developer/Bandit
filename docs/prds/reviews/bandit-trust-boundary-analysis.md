# Bandit Trust Boundary & Telemetry: Analysis and Recommendations

**Status:** Analysis input, not a PRD. This document is written to feed a separate PRD-shaping conversation. It records findings, a recommended design direction (the compensatory trust boundary), an explicit keep / modify / drop assessment against the current repository, an abstract telemetry data contract, and a build-versus-adopt recommendation for the supporting infrastructure. It does not define acceptance criteria, work breakdown, or schedule; those belong to the PRD that follows.

**Baseline reviewed:** `github.com/pyxe-developer/Bandit` at `main` (392 commits), specifically the founding architecture, the improvement metrics catalog, the glossary (`CONTEXT.md`), the input-quarantine policy and validator, and the CLI command surface in `src/`. Where this document says a capability "exists," it means it is present in the committed design corpus and, where noted, wired into the CLI; where it says "specified but not built," it means the concept is in the docs/glossary but not yet enforced in code. The README's "Current Status" (init/validate/list/show) is stale relative to the committed `src/cli.ts`, which already dispatches `risk-classification`, `auto-land-check`, `coderabbit-review`, `escalated-review`, `supply-chain-gate`, `input-quarantine`, `uat`, `improvements`, `coordination`, and more. This document trusts the code surface over the README.

## 1. Purpose and scope

The objective is to specify, at the analysis level, how Bandit decides **how much of a change can land without a human in the loop**, how that decision **moves over time on evidence**, and what **telemetry** the system must emit so that those decisions can be measured and attributed back to their origin.

Three things are explicitly in scope: the trust-boundary decision model (compensatory), the keep/modify/drop reconciliation against the current architecture, and the telemetry/evidence-source data contract. Three things are explicitly out of scope: the local-inference hardware plan (substrate, not trust logic), multi-user and external-contributor security (a later completeness, not this one), and the PRD-level work breakdown.

## 2. What the repository already establishes

The current architecture is further along conceptually than the README admits, and most of the trust-boundary machinery already has a home. The founding architecture defines a Landing Agent that "owns code-safety judgment under policy" and may auto-land chores and UAT-approved slices after a fixed gate sequence: CI/tests pass, the CodeRabbit pre-landing loop completes, local Qwen adversarial review completes, escalated adversarial review completes when smells require it, CodeRabbit findings are repaired or block, feature slices carry non-stale UAT, and a Landing Verdict is written. Escalation today is **smell-based**: a defined list of sensitive touch-points (auth, security, privacy, data integrity, schema, migration, infra, deploy, release, billing, workflow gates, approval systems, UAT, evidence freshness, state machines, parsers, validators, JSONL logs, registries, indexes, generated schemas) plus scope and process smells (large scope, low-confidence Qwen, request-changes, stale review/UAT/source drift, repeated repair loops, missing coverage, context pressure) raise review depth or block auto-landing.

The improvement engine already names the right raw materials. The metrics catalog defines `escaped_defect_count` as "defects found after landing that should have been caught," `blocked_landing_count`, `finding_acceptance_rate`, `false_positive_rate`, `cross_model_tension_validated_rate`, and the four improvement outcomes `keep` / `revise` / `revert` / `double_down`, with evaluation windows. The glossary supplies the discipline around changing the workflow safely: Workflow Trials, Predeclared Trial Decision Criteria, Minimum Detectable Effect Context, and the Trial Re-Evaluation Window. The evidence model supplies provenance and freshness: the Review Subject Hash, Hash-Based Evidence Freshness, Evidence SLO, Artifact Freshness Budget, and the append-only transition ledger. State is repo-native and canonical with a rebuildable SQLite index, and the CLI is the sole authority for state changes and landing decisions.

The single most important fact for everything below: the founding architecture states the current safety posture plainly — Bandit is designed for "cooperative local workflow safety: preventing operator slips, agent drift, stale approvals, missing review, and workflow forgetfulness," and explicitly **not** for "adversarial security against compromised credentials, malicious maintainers, or forged repository history." That stated posture is correct for a solo, self-owned-repo operator, and it is the anchor that determines what to keep and what to defer.

## 3. The decision direction: a compensatory trust boundary

The recommended model is **compensatory**: sufficiently strong, independent evidence can buy down the autonomy cost of a moderately risky change, rather than every risk signal acting only as a one-way veto that raises scrutiny. This is a deliberate departure from the current architecture, which is written as a smell-based escalation model — signals can only ratchet review *up*. The two models are not variants; they encode opposite answers to one question: can evidence compensate for risk, or only ever fail to relieve it? The escalation model is blunter and structurally cannot be argued into a risky landing; the compensatory model recovers more autonomy but introduces a failure class the escalation model lacks — being talked into a bad landing because the evidence looked strong. The compensatory model is the right target precisely because the goal is autonomy that ships software, and a pure veto lattice tends to converge on "escalate everything," which reproduces the human bottleneck. The cost of adopting it is that the safety burden shifts onto two things being genuinely true: that evidence strength is measured by *independence*, and that the boundary contracts fast when it is wrong.

### 3.1 The decision as a function of two axes

The landing decision is one function: given a change and its accumulated evidence, return an **autonomy level**. The autonomy levels form a ladder rather than a binary: *block* (cannot land), *human-review* (agent did the work, a human must read and approve), *notify-and-revert* (lands automatically but is flagged for asynchronous human spot-check and is cheaply reversible), and *auto-land* (no human in the loop). The assignment is the product of two independent axes.

The **risk axis** answers "how bad if this is wrong?" and is composed from signals Bandit already computes: blast radius, surface sensitivity (the smell catalog's sensitive touch-points), and reversibility. These collapse into a small number of named tiers — trivial, low, moderate, high — plus one category that sits outside the ladder entirely: the never-delegate surfaces, which route to a human regardless of evidence because their cost-if-wrong is unbounded or irreversible.

The **evidence axis** answers "how confident that it is right?" and — this is the load-bearing idea — its strength is a function of **independence, not quantity**. Five confirmations that share a blind spot are worth one. Two consequences shape the tiers directly. First, a test written by the same agent that wrote the code is weak evidence; a test that existed first, failed, and then passed (the architecture's red evidence) is strong, because it is independent of the implementation. Second, a same-model self-review is not an independent check; a different-model adversarial review instructed to hunt for failure is, because its error modes are decorrelated from the author's. The evidence tiers therefore order by accumulated decorrelated confirmation: author assertion only (never sufficient beyond trivial), plus author-controlled checks, plus independent automated confirmation (red-green plus static analysis plus CodeRabbit), plus decorrelated adversarial confirmation that sought failure and found none, plus human spot-check or a proven track record of the same change-class.

The trust boundary is the contour across these two axes where achieved evidence meets or exceeds the evidence *required* for that risk tier. Conservative initial contour, stated in prose rather than asserted as final: at trivial risk a single independent automated pass earns auto-land; at low risk auto-land requires independent confirmation and weaker evidence earns only notify-and-revert; at moderate risk auto-land requires the decorrelated adversarial reviewer to have looked and found nothing, and anything weaker routes to human-review; at high risk autonomy caps at notify-and-revert under the strongest evidence and most changes require human-review; never-delegate surfaces always require a human. This grid — risk tier against evidence tier, with an autonomy level in each cell — is the explicit, versioned encoding of "what is safe to land," and it is the object the learning loop edits.

### 3.2 The ratchet: how the boundary moves, safely

A permanently conservative boundary just reproduces the human bottleneck, so the boundary must move outward — but only on the system's own track record, and with a deliberate asymmetry: **fast contraction on failure, slow expansion on success**. Each grant of autonomy is implicitly a falsifiable prediction ("changes of this risk at this evidence level are correct"); record it at landing time, tagged to the cell that authorized it. Then measure outcomes via escape detection (section 5), and govern each cell by its **escape rate** — of changes that landed under that cell, what fraction later proved wrong. A cell may loosen only when its observed escape rate has held below a threshold declared *in advance*, over a sample large enough to distinguish the rate from noise (the Minimum Detectable Effect discipline already in the glossary). A cell tightens automatically and without debate the moment its escape rate breaches threshold. The asymmetry is the safety argument: over-cautious expansion costs only time, which the loop recovers; over-eager expansion ships a regression that destroys the trust the layer exists to produce. The Trial Re-Evaluation Window guards against the AI-specific failure where a cell that was safe becomes unsafe because the agent learns to produce changes that *look* like the safe class without being it (Goodhart pressure once a measure becomes an autonomy target).

## 4. Keep / Modify / Drop against the current architecture

This is a consolidation and sharpening of existing concepts plus one genuinely new subsystem, not a rewrite. The calls below are grounded in the committed design corpus and CLI surface.

### Keep as-is

The CLI-as-authority principle, repo-native canonical state, and the rebuildable SQLite index stay exactly as they are; they are the correct foundation and the compensatory model depends on the landing decision living in the CLI. The append-only transition ledger, the Review Subject Hash, Hash-Based Evidence Freshness, the Evidence SLO, and the Artifact Freshness Budget stay; they already provide the provenance and freshness primitives the data contract needs. The Workflow Trials machinery — Predeclared Trial Decision Criteria, Minimum Detectable Effect Context, Trial Re-Evaluation Window — stays unchanged in concept; the only change is pointing it at a new subject (boundary cells) rather than only at general workflow changes. The red-evidence stage stays and becomes load-bearing as the canonical example of independence-weighted evidence. The cross-model Adversarial Review Gate with the local-Qwen baseline stays; it is the decorrelated confirmation the evidence axis depends on. The smell catalog stays — but is repurposed (see modify). The founding safety posture (cooperative, not adversarial) stays as the explicit boundary of *this* completeness; the document should restate it so the compensatory model is not mistaken for a security claim.

### Modify

The escalation model becomes a **compensatory contour**. This is the central modification. Today the smell catalog and risk signals can only raise review depth or block; under the recommendation they become *inputs to a risk tier* that is then weighed against an *evidence tier*, so that strong independent evidence can reduce required human involvement on moderate-risk changes. The smell catalog is not dropped — it is reclassified from "veto triggers" into "risk-tier signals," with the sensitive-surface subset promoted to the never-delegate category that no evidence can override.

The improvement engine is modified from measuring *workflow changes* to also measuring *boundary cells*. `escaped_defect_count` is currently a global counter; it must be **decomposed per cell** and joined to the authorizing decision, becoming an escape *rate* per cell rather than an aggregate count. The keep/revise/revert/double-down outcomes, today applied to improvement chores, gain a second application: automatic tightening of a cell on threshold breach, and trial-gated loosening on sustained low escape rate.

The Landing Verdict artifact is modified to record the **prediction**: which cell authorized the landing, the risk tier and evidence tier assigned, and the evidence sources relied upon. Today the verdict records that gates passed; it must additionally record what it *bet* would hold, because that tag is the join key between a landing and a later escape.

The metrics catalog gains attribution dimensions (section 5) on the metrics it already lists, rather than new metrics — `escaped_defect_count`, `blocked_landing_count`, `model_cost_estimate`, and the repair-loop counts all need to carry the identity and provenance of the decision and actor they describe.

### Drop

Almost nothing should be dropped; the architecture's instincts are sound. Two things should be *retired in framing* rather than in code. First, the implication in the escalation model that scrutiny is monotonic (only ever increases) is dropped, since the compensatory model allows evidence to reduce it. Second, any assumption that `escaped_defect_count` as a single global number is sufficient for improvement decisions is dropped; an unattributed global count cannot govern a per-cell boundary and would let a safe cell and an unsafe cell average into a misleading middle. Nothing in the security posture is dropped — the deferral of adversarial/multi-party security is reaffirmed, not removed.

## 5. The telemetry and evidence-source data contract

The boundary and its ratchet are only as good as the data that feeds them, and the repository's current telemetry stops at counting. The gap is **attribution**: tying every measured outcome back to the decision that produced it and everything that decision rested on. This section defines that requirement abstractly, as a contract any implementation must satisfy, before naming a concrete implementation.

### 5.1 Why this is the hard part

Two cruxes carry the difficulty, and they are likely why a complete trust layer remains an unfilled gap. The first is **escape detection** — establishing ground truth about whether a landed change was actually wrong, which arrives late and indirectly (a later revert touching the same lines, a hotfix, downstream test breakage, an incident, a human quietly rewriting it). A boundary that cannot reliably learn it was wrong cannot safely loosen; "no complaints" is not "no errors." The second is **independence preservation** — the evidence tiers assume the adversarial reviewer is decorrelated from the author; if it shares the author's model family or sees the author's rationale, an evidence tier labeled "independent" is one opinion wearing two hats. Disproportionate effort belongs on these two, not on the contour bookkeeping.

### 5.2 The contract (abstract, implementation-independent)

Every model call, every tool call, and every landing decision must emit a record sufficient to reconstruct, after the fact, *who decided what, on what basis, and what happened next*. Concretely, the contract requires the following to be captured and durably stored as repo-reconstructable evidence:

For every **model call**: a stable actor identity (which agent/role), the model and version actually used, the agent profile in force (a hash of prompt, tool allowlist, permission scope, budget, timeout), the work item, the call's purpose (author, reviewer, escalation), token and cost accounting, and a request/response capture or its hash. The independence requirement is explicit: a reviewer call must record whether it had access to the author's reasoning, and the author and reviewer identities must be comparable so decorrelation can be verified rather than assumed.

For every **tool call**: the actor identity, the tool invoked, its inputs and outputs (or hashes), success/failure, and the network destination or surface touched, so that supply-chain-sensitive actions are attributable.

For every **landing decision**: the authorizing cell, the assigned risk tier and evidence tier, the specific evidence artifacts relied upon (by Review Subject Hash), the predicted outcome the cell implies, the autonomy level granted, and the operator involvement if any. This is the falsifiable prediction record.

For **escape detection**: a durable link from any later corrective event (revert, hotfix, downstream failure, incident, post-hoc human flag) back to the landing decision and cell that allowed the original change, with enough signal to compute a per-cell escape rate over an evaluation window.

The unifying property is a **join key**: actor identity, model+version, profile hash, evidence-source hashes, and authorizing cell must travel together on every record, so outcomes can be attributed to decisions and decisions to their provenance. The records must be repo-reconstructable (consistent with canonical repo-native state) and tamper-evident enough that an agent cannot quietly rewrite the evidence of its own work.

### 5.3 What this adds beyond today's metrics

The metrics catalog already names most of the *quantities*; the contract adds the *dimensions* that make them governable: identity, model+version, profile hash, evidence provenance, and authorizing cell attached to each record. `escaped_defect_count` becomes attributable and therefore decomposable into per-cell escape rates; `model_cost_estimate` becomes attributable to actor and profile and therefore enforceable as a budget; cross-model tension becomes checkable for genuine independence. No new metric families are required to start — the existing V0 metrics gain attribution.

## 6. Build it yourself versus adopt Aperture + Tailscale now

You asked that the contract be abstract and that Aperture + Tailscale be named as a recommended, swappable implementation, with a stated preference for building without external dependency if the lift is acceptable — but with explicit weight given to whether adopting now unblocks other stalled projects while you build and swap later. Here is that weighing.

**What the contract needs, reduced to essentials:** identity-stamped capture of every model and tool call; a single place that holds provider credentials so keys are not scattered into images and env files; per-actor cost accounting and enforceable budgets; and durable, tamper-evident, repo-reconstructable evidence records. Notably, almost all of this lives at the **model-call boundary** and the **evidence store**, both of which are squarely inside Bandit's own CLI authority — which means a self-built implementation is genuinely feasible and does not violate any founding principle.

**The self-built path.** Route every agent's model calls through a thin local proxy or wrapper that Bandit owns, which stamps identity/model/profile onto each call, accounts cost, enforces budget, and writes the capture into the existing `.bandit/` evidence store as JSONL keyed by the join key. Escape detection is built as a CLI command that scans git history and CI outcomes to link corrective events back to landing verdicts. The lift here is moderate and concentrated in two places: the per-call interception wrapper (well-understood) and escape detection (the genuinely hard part — but it is hard *regardless* of who builds it, and no external product solves it for you, because it is specific to your repos and your definition of "defect"). The advantage is zero external dependency, full alignment with the Runtime Portability Gate, and an artifact that is entirely your own — which matters for the open-source-credibility goal. The disadvantage is that you build the credential custody, the capture pipeline, and the cost accounting yourself, which is real but not large work.

**The adopt-now path (Aperture + Tailscale).** Aperture by Tailscale already provides, today, the identity-bound model-call gateway, centralized provider-key custody, full session capture exportable to durable storage, per-user and team spend quotas, and a pre-request hook that can block or modify a call — which is most of the contract's model-call half, delivered as configuration rather than code. Tailscale supplies the private network and the identity-stamped flow/audit logs. This path lets you satisfy the model-call boundary and the cost-control requirement in days, not weeks, with no bespoke proxy to maintain — freeing your attention to spend on the parts no product solves (the contour and escape detection) and, critically, on the **other stalled projects**. The disadvantages are real and should be stated: Aperture is alpha with unpublished GA pricing, so it carries product risk and a future cost unknown; it has a documented gap (MCP tool-call authorization is enforced at tool-list time, not call time); and adopting it now means a later swap if you decide to internalize the boundary — which the abstract contract is specifically designed to make cheap.

**Recommendation.** Treat the data contract as the durable artifact and the implementation as swappable, exactly as you framed it. Given the explicit weight on unblocking stalled work, the pragmatic sequence is: **adopt Aperture + Tailscale now for the model-call boundary and cost control** (fast, frees you to ship the stalled projects and to concentrate on the hard core), **build escape detection and the boundary contour yourself from the start** (no product does this, and it is the moat), and **keep the self-built proxy as a known, low-risk fallback** that the contract makes a swap rather than a rebuild. The deciding factor is opportunity cost: the self-built model-call boundary is a few weeks you could instead spend on the contour, escape detection, and your stalled projects, and the contract guarantees you are not locked in. If Aperture's alpha instability or pricing becomes a problem, the fallback is already specified. The one thing you should *not* outsource under any circumstance — to Aperture or anyone — is escape detection and the boundary logic, because that is the unfilled gap and therefore the thing worth owning.

## 8. Summary of the recommendation in one paragraph

Adopt a compensatory trust boundary expressed as an explicit, versioned risk-by-evidence contour, where evidence strength is measured by independence and the boundary moves outward only on the system's own escape-rate track record, contracting fast and expanding slow. This is a consolidation and sharpening of what Bandit already has — the smell catalog becomes risk-tier input rather than veto, the improvement engine measures cells rather than only workflows, and the Landing Verdict records a falsifiable prediction — plus one genuinely new subsystem, escape detection, which is the hard, unfilled core and the thing to own. Define telemetry abstractly as a join-keyed data contract over every model call, tool call, and landing decision; satisfy the model-call and cost-control half by adopting Aperture + Tailscale now to unblock stalled work, while building escape detection and the boundary yourself and keeping a self-built proxy as a specified, low-cost fallback. Preserve the founding posture: this is cooperative trust for AI-authored code that ships, not adversarial multi-party security, and that boundary is deliberate.
